The quarter in one paragraph

Summary

Netflix reported Q2 2026 revenue of $12.56 billion, up 13.4% year over year but marginally below the $12.59 billion consensus. Diluted EPS of $0.80 edged past the $0.79 estimate, up 11% from $0.72 in Q2 2025, with net income of $3.40 billion. Operating income was $4.2 billion, up 11%, though operating margin compressed slightly to 33.4% from 34.1% a year ago. Netflix no longer reports paid net subscriber additions — global membership stands at 325 million. The ad-supported tier reached 250 million monthly active viewers, and full-year ad revenue remains on track for roughly $3 billion, about double 2025 levels. For Q3, Netflix guided to 12% revenue growth and a 33.2% operating margin — the latter a roughly 500 basis point improvement over Q3 2025's 28.2% — and narrowed full-year revenue guidance to $51.0–$51.4 billion. Shares fell approximately 9% after hours to a 52-week low as the market focused on the small revenue miss and the third consecutive quarter of decelerating top-line growth rather than the margin improvement embedded in guidance.

1 · Revenue — $12.56B

Growth decelerated from 16.2% in Q1 to 13.4% in Q2, with Q3 guided to ~12%. The miss was ~$30 million on a $12.5 billion quarter — 0.25%, irrelevant in isolation, but the market read it against the deceleration trend. EMEA crossed $4.0B in quarterly revenue for the first time; LATAM and APAC each crossed $1.5B; all four regions grew double digits.

Source: Netflix Q2 2026 Shareholder Letter & Press Release (ir.netflix.net); consensus via CNBC (secondary).

2 · Diluted EPS — $0.80

A clean beat with no one-time items of the scale seen in Q1 2026 (the $2.8B Warner Bros. termination fee). Net income of $3.40 billion grew 8.6% year over year. Operationally this was a tidier quarter than Q1 — which is partly why the market's reaction traced to guidance, not results.

Source: Netflix Q2 2026 Press Release (ir.netflix.net); earnings call transcript.

3 · Operating margin — 33.4%

Down 70 bps year over year, and the least important margin number in the report. The Q3 guide of 33.2% against Q3 2025's 28.2% is the one that matters: a ~500 basis point year-over-year improvement implies real operating leverage arriving exactly as revenue growth slows. Content amortization growth was characterized as peaking in Q2 and decelerating through H2.

Source: Netflix Q2 2026 Shareholder Letter (ir.netflix.net); earnings call commentary.

4 · Ad tier — 250M monthly active viewers

Disclosed at the May 2026 Upfront and reiterated in context this quarter: ad revenue remains on track for ~$3 billion in 2026, roughly double 2025. Netflix does not break out quarterly ad revenue, but 250M MAUs is larger advertising reach than most traditional TV networks — the structural story sitting inside a report the market judged on deceleration.

Source: Netflix May 2026 Upfront presentation; Q2 2026 earnings call commentary.

5 · Q3 guidance — 12% revenue growth, 33.2% operating margin

The number that moved the stock. Netflix narrowed full-year revenue guidance to $51.0–$51.4B (from $50.7–$51.7B) with the midpoint roughly flat. Q3's implied ~$13.0B continues the sequential deceleration — 16% → 13% → 12% — and the market was pricing in either re-acceleration or a more constructive guide. It got neither, and shares fell ~9% after hours.

Source: Netflix Q2 2026 Shareholder Letter (ir.netflix.net); market reaction via Variety/Deadline (secondary).

Quarter over quarter

What changed

Growth — third consecutive quarter of deceleration

16.2% in Q1 2026, 13.4% in Q2, ~12% guided for Q3. Not a collapse — every region still grew double digits — but the trajectory, not the level, is what the market repriced.

Disclosure — subscriber additions are gone

This is the first full quarter with no paid net additions figure. Membership is disclosed as a point-in-time 325 million. Demand visibility now runs through revenue, engagement hours, and ad MAUs — a deliberate re-anchoring of the story from subscriber counts to monetization.

Margins — Q2 dipped, Q3 guide jumped

Q2 operating margin of 33.4% was down 70 bps year over year, but Q3 is guided to 33.2% against 28.2% in Q3 2025 — a ~500 bps improvement. Content amortization growth was described as peaking in Q2 and decelerating through H2, which supports the margin path into year-end.

Guidance — narrowed, not lowered

Full-year revenue guidance tightened to $51.0–$51.4B from $50.7–$51.7B, midpoint roughly unchanged. The range compressed; the outlook did not deteriorate — a distinction most headline coverage skipped.

Engagement — modest growth on a bigger base

H1 2026 viewing totaled 97 billion hours, up about 2% year over year. Engagement is growing far slower than revenue — monetization per hour, via pricing and advertising, is doing the work.

Regions — EMEA crossed $4B for the first time

EMEA passed $4.0B in quarterly revenue; LATAM and APAC each crossed $1.5B. All four regions grew double digits, keeping this a broad-based rather than single-region story.

The two reads — and the one most people skip

Bull, bear & overlooked

Bull read

  • The Q3 margin guide is the definition of operating leverage. 33.2% guided against 28.2% a year ago means profit is set to grow faster than revenue even as the top line slows.
  • The ad business is a second engine that barely existed three years ago. 250M MAUs and ~$3B in 2026 ad revenue, doubling year over year, with pricing power still early.
  • Guidance was narrowed, not cut. A tighter range around an unchanged midpoint, with double-digit growth in all four regions and content costs past their peak growth rate.

Bear read

  • Three straight quarters of deceleration. 16% → 13% → 12% guided, and the market has no subscriber-adds metric left to check the trajectory against.
  • Engagement is nearly flat. 97 billion H1 hours, up only ~2% — revenue growth is being manufactured from price and ads, not from people watching more.
  • A 52-week low on a beat quarter is a sentiment signal. When a 0.25% revenue miss produces a 9% drawdown, the multiple was priced for perfection.

What everyone might miss

The market sold the deceleration and skipped the margin inflection

Nearly all coverage led with the growth slowdown and the after-hours drop. The Q3 operating margin guide — 33.2% versus 28.2% a year earlier — was treated as a footnote. A ~500 basis point year-over-year margin improvement, guided in advance, alongside content amortization growth peaking, is the profile of a company shifting from growth-at-any-cost to cash compounding.

The angle for a newsletter or thread is not “Netflix fell 9%.” It is: “the market repriced Netflix on a metric Netflix no longer reports, while ignoring the one management actually guided up.” That tension — deceleration versus operating leverage — is the debate worth owning this cycle.

Newsletter section

Netflix Q2 2026: The Market Repriced a Metric Netflix No Longer Reports

Netflix reported $12.56 billion in Q2 revenue, up 13% year over year but about $30 million short of consensus — a 0.25% miss. EPS of $0.80 beat estimates, and net income reached $3.40 billion. Shares still fell roughly 9% after hours to a 52-week low, because the market's attention was on trajectory, not results: growth has slowed from 16% in Q1 to 13% in Q2, with Q3 guided at 12%.

Two things complicate the bearish read. First, the Q3 operating margin guide of 33.2% sits roughly 500 basis points above Q3 2025's 28.2% — profit is set to grow meaningfully faster than revenue, with content amortization growth described as peaking in Q2 and easing through the second half. Second, the advertising business keeps scaling: 250 million monthly active viewers on the ad-supported tier and roughly $3 billion in 2026 ad revenue, about double last year. Netflix also narrowed full-year revenue guidance to $51.0–$51.4 billion around an unchanged midpoint — tightened, not cut.

The structural note: this was the first full quarter without a reported subscriber-additions figure. Membership stands at 325 million, and the company now wants to be judged on revenue, margins, and ad reach. The market, for one afternoon at least, judged it on the metric it no longer publishes.
